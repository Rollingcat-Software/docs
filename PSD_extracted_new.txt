PROBLEM STATEMENT Authentication processes have become an integral part of our daily lives with the proliferation of e-government systems, online banking, healthcare portals, public transportation systems and smart buildings. However, widely used methods such as passwords, access cards, or single-factor biometric systems harbor various security risks and usability issues. Passwords can be stolen, cards can be cloned, and simple biometric systems can be easily circumvented by spoofing attacks using static photos or videos. This situation creates significant security gaps for both individual users and corporate entities, establishing the clear importance and worth of developing more robust solutions. 2. PROBLEM DESCRIPTION AND MOTIVATION Current biometric systems lack the sophisticated liveness detection capabilities necessary to combat increasingly sophisticated spoofing attempts. The absence of multi-modal biometric fusion leaves these systems vulnerable to presentation attacks, while their centralized data storage architectures present attractive targets for cyber criminals. The integration challenges between physical access control systems and digital authentication platforms result in fragmented security ecosystems that are difficult to manage and monitor comprehensively. From a distributed systems perspective, traditional authentication services face significant performance bottlenecks when processing high volumes of concurrent verification requests. The lack of proper caching strategies, absence of load balancing mechanisms, and inefficient database query patterns lead to increased latency and degraded user experience. Legacy authentication systems violate fundamental software engineering principles, making them difficult to maintain and extend. The violation of the Single Responsibility Principle results in authentication services that handle everything from user management to audit logging, creating unwieldy codebases. The lack of proper abstraction layers and dependency inversion makes it nearly impossible to swap out components or integrate new biometric modalities without extensive refactoring. These systems often lack comprehensive API documentation, making third-party integration challenging and error-prone. The primary motivation for this project is to create a secure, flexible, and user-friendly platform that consolidates these fragmented and insecure authentication methods. We plan to develop a cloud-native Software as a Service (SaaS) platform targeting B2B (Business-to-Business) and B2B2C (Business-to-Business-to-Consumer) markets. The project aims to unify both physical (e.g., opening doors) and digital (e.g., logging into systems) access control for a wide range of applications, from residential complexes to corporate offices. The platform is intended to differentiate itself and fill a significant market gap through its unique liveness detection mechanism against fraud and its high accuracy achieved by fusing multiple biometric modalities. 3. MAIN GOAL AND OBJECTIVES The main goal of the project is to design, develop, and present a prototype of a multi-tenant, cloud-native, multi-platform system providing a multi-biometric identity authentication SaaS platform that serves as a modern, secure, and flexible alternative to traditional authentication systems. The specific objectives set to achieve this main goal are as follows: To design and develop a secure and scalable backend infrastructure. This infrastructure will consist of a main service (Identity Core API) responsible for user, tenant, and role management, and a specialized service (Biometric Processor API) for handling computationally intensive biometric operations, in accordance with microservices architecture principles. To develop an innovative and active liveness detection algorithm, the &quot;Biometric Puzzle,&quot; which requires the user to complete a randomly generated sequence of actions (e.g., smiling, blinking) to prevent spoofing attacks using static photos, video recordings, or masks. To integrate high-accuracy face recognition and face verification capabilities into the system using proven deep learning models, such as those provided by the DeepFace library, and to evaluate their performance. To develop a user-friendly mobile application for both Android and iOS platforms that allows end-users to enroll their biometric data and authenticate their identity. To design the necessary multi-tenant data model for the system to operate as a SaaS platform and to ensure that each tenant's data is completely isolated at the PostgreSQL database level. 4. RELATED WORK The field of automated identity verification and biometric authentication has progressed rapidly, driven by advances in deep learning, liveness detection, and cloud-native deployment. Foundational frameworks such as DeepFace and FaceNet demonstrated that deep networks could encode facial images into numerical embeddings for comparison, establishing the basis of modern face recognition. However, these systems were limited to single-device operation without scalability, modularity, or compliance with enterprise requirements. Our project extends these principles into a multi-service, distributed platform designed for real-time verification, data isolation, and tenant-based scalability. Significant model-level improvements emerged with ArcFace , which introduced angular margin loss for more discriminative facial embeddings. Further developments such as AdaFace and MagFace improved robustness under low-quality and off-angle conditions through adaptive margin learning. While these methods enhanced verification accuracy, they mainly addressed algorithmic precision rather than system-level deployment. Our implementation combines these approaches through InsightFace , embedding them in a microservices architecture where the biometric inference engine operates independently of identity management, supporting concurrent and secure operations across multiple tenants. Beyond recognition accuracy, liveness detection remains essential to prevent spoofing. Recent studies using ResNet-18 or MobileNetV3 architectures with AdMSoftmax Loss achieved high detection accuracy for presentation attacks, but most function as isolated models. Our system merges both passive texture-based and active challenge–response verification, integrating ResNet-18 and Google MediaPipe in a two-stage liveness pipeline. This hybrid approach ensures resilience against photo, video, and deepfake attacks with minimal latency. In addition to academic research, industrial solutions have advanced the field. Microsoft Azure Face Liveness Detection offers PAD-compliant active and passive verification; Amazon Rekognition Face Liveness analyzes selfie videos to detect spoofs via deepfake and mask resistance; and Sodec Technologies (Türkiye) provides ID verification with passive liveness for e-government and banking applications. FaceAuth Vision , an open-source OpenCV-based system, demonstrates offline flexibility but lacks industrial-grade spoof detection, while BioGATE Pass (Proline) integrates NFC chip reading under ICAO compliance for high-trust ID verification. Compared to these systems, our project aims to bridge the gap between commercial reliability and open research flexibility by providing a cloud-native, multi-tenant, and multi-platform SaaS framework. It integrates both active and passive liveness detection, multi-biometric fusion, and official ID photo-based verification within a scalable microservices architecture. This approach ensures alignment with ISO/IEC PAD standards, supports customizable deployments for privacy-conscious institutions, and offers a transparent alternative to proprietary cloud solutions through open-source technologies. SCOPE The scope of this project includes the development of a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) of the platform, encompassing its core functionalities. The items that are within and outside the scope of this project are clearly defined below. 5.1. In Scope Backend Development: Development of the Identity Core API and Biometric Processor API microservices using Spring Boot and FastAPI. Database Architecture: Design of a multi-tenant data model on PostgreSQL, including user, role, and tenant tables, and the storage of biometric vectors using the pgvector extension. Face Biometrics: Integration of face recognition (1:N) and face verification (1:1) functionalities using the DeepFace library. Liveness Detection: Development of the on-device &quot;Biometric Puzzle&quot; algorithm using Google MediaPipe. Mobile Application: Development of basic user interfaces for both Android and iOS platforms using Flutter, enabling end-user enrollment and authentication. Infrastructure: Containerization of the entire system using Docker and Docker Compose for local development and execution. 5.2. Out of Scope Hardware Production: Design and manufacturing of dedicated Edge devices for physical access control are excluded. These interactions will be simulated using a Raspberry Pi or Jetson Nano. 5.3. Constraints Hardware Constraints: The performance of the verification and liveness detection algorithm will be limited by the processing power and camera quality of the mobile devices used for testing. Data Constraints: The training of the face recognition model is out of scope. The system's accuracy will be limited by the performance of the pre-trained models provided by the DeepFace library. Image Quality Constraint: For optimal performance in face recognition and liveness detection, input images should have a resolution higher than 480p. Lower-quality images may reduce detection accuracy and reliability. 5.4. Assumptions It is assumed that stable versions of the chosen open-source libraries (DeepFace, MediaPipe, Spring, FastAPI) will be accessible and compatible throughout the project lifecycle. It is assumed that the mobile devices used for testing meet the minimum hardware requirements for real-time video analysis. It is assumed that the team members possess the fundamental competencies in the selected technologies (Java, Python, Flutter, Docker) and are capable of acquiring any necessary additional knowledge during the project. 6. METHODOLOGY AND TECHNICAL APPROACH The system employs a microservices-based architecture that ensures modularity, scalability, and fault isolation while forming a cloud-native multi-biometric SaaS platform with maintainability, resilience, and secure tenant isolation. 6.1. High-Level System Architecture The platform follows a distributed microservices model operating behind a unified API Gateway. Services communicate via REST APIs for synchronous requests and Redis-based message queues for asynchronous workflows. The data layer combines relational and vector databases for structured and biometric data management. Identity Core API (Spring Boot): Manages authentication, authorization, tenant administration, and security policies under a Hexagonal Architecture . Biometric Processor API (FastAPI): Handles compute-intensive AI workloads such as feature extraction, similarity matching, and liveness analysis. Database Layer: Uses PostgreSQL for transactional data and the pgvector extension for similarity search between high-dimensional biometric embeddings. Mobile Layer: Enables enrollment and authentication while running on-device liveness detection for privacy and low latency. Infrastructure Layer: All services are containerized with Docker Compose for local execution and remain portable to Kubernetes for future horizontal scaling. 6.2. Technical Approach and Algorithms 6.2.1. Backend Services The backend comprises two main microservices aligned with SOLID and Domain-Driven Design (DDD) principles: Identity Core API (Spring Boot): Acts as the platform’s central orchestrator, handling user, role, and tenant management, JWT-based security, and integration with the Biometric Processor API. Data persistence is achieved through Spring Data JPA, while request validation and rate limiting ensure API robustness. Biometric Processor API (FastAPI): Built in Python to leverage the machine learning ecosystem, this service performs face recognition, verification, and liveness detection. It exposes REST endpoints for embedding generation, similarity comparison, and anti-spoofing checks. The service utilizes DeepFace for deep embedding extraction and Redis as a message queue to support concurrent inference quests. This architecture allows the computational workload to scale independently from business logic, improving performance under concurrent authentication events. -78104 4211955 -78104 8514715 Figure 1: Flow Chart of Our System -78104 8514715 6.2.2. Biometric Puzzle (Liveness Detection) Algorithm Liveness detection runs on the mobile device to improve responsiveness and privacy through an active challenge–response combined with passive texture cues : The backend sends random challenges (e.g., blink, smile, turn head). The app captures video via OpenCV and processes frames with Google MediaPipe to track 468 facial landmarks. Metrics like Eye Aspect Ratio (EAR) and Mouth Aspect Ratio (MAR) detect specific actions. Once all challenges succeed, the app confirms liveness and securely transmits the validated frame to the backend. This hybrid approach prevents spoofing via photos, video replays, or deepfakes by requiring genuine facial motion and temporal consistency. Figure 2: Face Landmarking 6.2.3. System Integration and Security All inter-service communication will occur over HTTPS with token-based authentication. Sensitive biometric embeddings are encrypted before storage, and role-based access control (RBAC) ensures that tenants can only access their isolated data domain. Continuous integration and testing pipelines verify API integrity after each deployment using Postman and Pytest/JUnit test suites. 6.3. Energy efficiency is prioritized to reduce the indirect impact of server and device usage. Ethical: Users are informed and provide explicit consent for biometric data processing. Algorithmic bias will be mitigated by testing with diverse datasets and monitoring fairness metrics. Health &amp; Safety: The system poses no physical risks. All personal and biometric data are encrypted both in transit (TLS) and at rest (AES-256). Sustainability: The modular microservices architecture and Docker-based deployment support maintainability and long-term scalability. Social: The platform enhances security and user experience while enforcing strict data access controls to prevent misuse for surveillance. 7.3. Legal considerations As the platform processes biometric data, it will comply with the Turkish Personal Data Protection Law (KVKK No. 6698) and the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). All open-source dependencies will be checked for license compatibility to prevent legal conflicts in future commercialization. Measurability/Measuring Success The success of the project will be evaluated based on the extent to which each objective defined in Section 3 is met. The success criteria for each objective are defined below: Objective 1: To design and develop a secure and scalable backend infrastructure. Success Factor: All microservices run smoothly on Docker, API endpoints pass Postman tests successfully, and basic user/tenant CRUD operations function as expected. Objective 2: To develop an innovative and active liveness detection algorithm (&quot;Biometric Puzzle&quot;). Success Factor: The developed algorithm can be completed by legitimate users with a success rate of over 95%, and it rejects simple spoofing attacks, such as static photos or videos held up to the camera, with a rate of over 99%. Objective 3: To integrate high-accuracy face recognition capabilities into the system. Success Factor: The system operates in a controlled test environment with a False Acceptance Rate (FAR) of less than 1% and a False Rejection Rate (FRR) of less than 5%. Objective 4: To develop a user-friendly mobile application. Success Factor: The mobile application runs stably on both Android and iOS devices, and an end-user can complete the enrollment and authentication flow smoothly in under 1 minute. Objective 5: To design the multi-tenant data model. Success Factor: Integration tests prove that a user belonging to one tenant cannot access the data of another tenant under any circumstances. 9.2. Risk Management Potential risks that may be encountered during the project and the contingency plans (Plan B) for these risks are listed below: Risk 1: Insufficient Technical Competency Description: Team members encounter more difficulty than expected with one of the selected technologies, slowing down the development process. Plan B: Undertake intensive online training and seek support from relevant technology communities (forums, Discord channels). If necessary, simplify the scope of the feature. Risk 2: Library Performance Issues Description: Google MediaPipe fails to deliver adequate performance for real-time analysis on the low-end devices used for testing. Plan B: Reduce the number of landmarks to be analyzed, decrease the resolution of the video stream, or reduce the number of steps in the &quot;Biometric Puzzle&quot; to lighten the processing load. Risk 3: Integration Problems Description: Unexpected issues arise in the communication between microservices written in different languages (Java and Python) or with the mobile application. Plan B: Define API contracts (OpenAPI) in great detail from the very beginning. Consider a stricter protocol for inter-service communication, such as gRPC. 10.
